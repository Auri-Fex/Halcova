[Living Language: Contemporary Slang and Idioms of Hálcova](living_language_slang.md)

Overview

Language in Hálcova reflects and reinforces social structures. The formal High Hálcovan taught in schools and used in official contexts differs significantly from the living speech of streets, taverns, and homes. Slang, idioms, and vernacular expressions reveal how ordinary people understand and navigate their world—often subverting official meanings while using official vocabulary.

This document catalogs contemporary informal language: the expressions pattern-privileged nobles use among themselves, the coded speech of criminals, the bitter humor of the constrained, and the regional variations that mark speakers' origins. Understanding this living language provides insight into Hálcovan culture beyond official pronouncements.

Pattern-Related Slang

The pattern system that structures society generates extensive vernacular vocabulary.

General Pattern Terms

Threadbare: Someone with severely constrained pattern status, used pejoratively. Implies poverty, limited rights, and low social position. "Don't trust him with the accounts—he's threadbare, desperate for any marks."

Golden thread: A pattern-privileged individual. Can be neutral description or express envy/resentment depending on speaker's tone and context. "She's golden thread, born into the second ring. Never worked a day collecting Residue like us."

Tangled: Confused, lost, or in complicated circumstances. References pattern threads becoming disordered. "I'm completely tangled on this contract—can't figure out who owes what to whom."

Woven tight: Well-connected, powerful, secure in one's position. Implies strong pattern status and social relationships. "The magistrate's woven tight with the Crown—don't expect justice in his court."

Loose end: An unreliable person, someone likely to cause problems. Also criminal slang for lowest-ranking members. "Keep your plans away from Marcus—he's a loose end waiting to unravel."

Frayed: Damaged, broken, mentally unstable. Implies pattern degradation affecting behavior or capability. "She's been frayed since her son's Correction. Just stares at nothing."

Smooth weave: Easy, uncomplicated, proceeding without problems. "The delivery went smooth weave—no inspections, no delays."

Knotted up: In a complicated or difficult situation. When used about people, implies entanglement with problems. "The whole negotiation's knotted up now that they discovered the forgery."

Cut from the cloth: Destined for something, pattern-determined to a particular fate. Often used ironically. "He's cut from the cloth for the arena—too stupid and aggressive for anything else."

Reading the threads: Understanding a situation clearly, seeing truth beneath surface appearances. "She's reading the threads on this deal—knows exactly who's cheating whom."

Status-Specific Terms

Ring-jumper: Someone attempting to rise above their pattern-assigned status. Pejorative from pattern-privileged perspective, sometimes admiring from below. "Another ring-jumper caught with forged certificates. They never learn."

Circle-bound: Stuck in one's assigned social position with no possibility of advancement. Resignation or complaint depending on context. "We're all circle-bound—born to this ring, die in this ring."

Status-stink: The indefinable quality that reveals someone's true pattern status despite attempts to appear otherwise. "No matter how fine his clothes, you can smell the status-stink on him."

Privilege-blind: Unable or unwilling to recognize advantages of pattern-privileged position. Common complaint from below. "The nobles are privilege-blind—think everyone can just buy their way out of trouble."

Loom and Fate Idioms

The Loom—the vast predictive apparatus underlying Hálcovan society—generates extensive idiomatic language.

Fate Acceptance

The Loom knows: Fate is inevitable, predetermined, beyond individual control. Can express genuine faith or bitter resignation. "Why fight it? The Loom knows what becomes of us."

Following the pattern: Accepting fate, living according to what's been determined. Can be virtue (from Church perspective) or criticism (implying passivity). "He's just following the pattern—never questioned anything in his life."

Loom's mercy: Expression of hope for favorable fate. "Loom's mercy we reach port before the storm hits."

Against the weave: Fighting against fate, attempting impossible change. Usually implies futility. "Trying to save the condemned is against the weave—you'll only join them."

Thread's end: Death approaching, fate concluding. "Old Márton's reached his thread's end. Won't see another harvest."

New thread: A fresh start, rebirth, possibility of change. Somewhat subversive implication that fate might be altered. "Maybe the new posting will be a new thread—chance to become someone different."

Observation and Scrutiny

Attendance is watching: Be careful, you're being observed by those who track patterns. References the Attendance—operators of the Loom's monitoring functions. "Attendance is watching—keep your opinions about the Church quiet."

Warden's eye: Under scrutiny by authorities, specifically pattern-enforcement. "He's been under Warden's eye since his brother's arrest. Every move watched."

Loom's blind spot: Somewhere or something unmonitored, outside normal observation. Valuable knowledge for criminals and dissidents. "The warehouse is in the Loom's blind spot—even Attendance can't see what happens there."

Thread-traced: Being tracked through pattern connections, unable to hide. "Once they thread-trace you, running's pointless. They'll find you anywhere."

Seasonal References

The five seasons shape temporal idioms:

Fávarzach-mad: Crazy, unpredictable, chaotic. References the fifth season's disruption of normal patterns. "The market's gone Fávarzach-mad—prices changing by the hour."

Évresá-green: Young, inexperienced, naive. References spring's new growth. "He's still Évresá-green, thinks hard work actually gets rewarded."

Hórvam-cold: Emotionally distant, unfeeling, calculating. References winter's chill. "The magistrate's Hórvam-cold—don't expect sympathy."

Fágvard-dark: Depressed, gloomy, pessimistic. References autumn's declining light. "She's been Fágvard-dark since the bankruptcy. Barely speaks."

Szárnyév-bright: Optimistic, cheerful, perhaps naively positive. References summer's heat and light. "Still Szárnyév-bright about the venture? Wait until the bills arrive."

Class-Based Slang

Different social strata speak distinctly, using vocabulary that identifies their position.

Pattern-Privileged Speech

Nobility and wealthy pattern-privileged use terminology that reinforces their status:

Beneath the weave: Lower classes, those below speaker's status. Dismissive. "Don't concern yourself with those beneath the weave—they have their place."

Common thread: An ordinary person, someone without distinction. "He married a common thread—his family's still recovering from the scandal."

Refined pattern: Cultured, educated, properly upper-class. "She displays a refined pattern—excellent education shows in everything she does."

Proper threading: Correct behavior according to upper-class standards. "Proper threading requires acknowledging one's inferiors without engaging them."

Unraveled: Socially ruined, reputation destroyed. Devastating for those dependent on social standing. "After the scandal, he's completely unraveled. No house will receive him."

Patterned up: Married into or otherwise acquired higher status. "She patterned up nicely—husband's family is third ring."

Working Class Speech

Merchants, craftsmen, and laborers use different vocabulary:

Marks and sparks: Money and trouble, the constants of working life. "It's all marks and sparks with him—chasing coin and running from problems."

Coin-counter: A merchant, often derogatory when used by non-merchants. "The coin-counters raised prices again. They'll bleed us dry."

Thread-puller: Someone who manipulates others for advantage. "Don't trust her—she's a thread-puller working every angle."

Warden-bait: Risky behavior likely to attract enforcement attention. "Selling those texts is Warden-bait. You'll hang."

Church-pocket: Someone who tithes heavily or displays excessive religious conformity. "He's a real Church-pocket—first at every service, loudest in every hymn."

Guild-groveler: One who seeks favor from guild authorities at expense of fellow workers. "The guild-grovelers reported the extra moonlighting. Lost three of us our positions."

Pattern-Constrained Speech

Those with constrained status develop coded and bitter vocabulary:

Golden bastards: Pattern-privileged individuals, hostile. "The golden bastards took our farm. Four generations, gone in a ruling."

Warden-food: Someone likely to be Corrected or executed. "Keep your mouth shut or you're Warden-food."

Free thread: Someone who has escaped pattern-constraint—legally through exceptional service, illegally through forgery or flight. Rare and admired. "They say he's a free thread now, living in the Reaches under a new name."

Residue-bait: Someone likely to die soon, either through dangerous work or pattern-enforcement. Dark humor. "New workers are just Residue-bait. Half won't last the season."

Loom-fucked: Doomed by fate, cursed by circumstances. Vulgar and common. "We're all Loom-fucked, born to this and dying to this."

Certificate-dreaming: Hoping for improved status through legitimate means. Usually ironic. "Still certificate-dreaming? Nobody gets promoted out of the fifth ring."

Hollow: Empty of pattern-control, free from observation. Either describes places outside monitoring or people who've escaped the system. "The mountain village is hollow—no Attendance, no Wardens, just people living."

Regional Slang

Regional identity shows in distinctive vocabulary.

Heartlands

The political and cultural center generates vocabulary that others often adopt:

Fonvárs fancy: Pretentious, putting on airs, aspiring above one's station. Used throughout Hálcova about capital affectations. "Look at him with his Fonvárs fancy manners—still reeks of the farm."

Crown's coin: Official, legitimate, properly authorized. "Make sure the shipment's Crown's coin—no unauthorized cargo."

Market-mouth: Gossip, unreliable rumor, speculation. "Don't believe market-mouth—half of what they say is invented."

Arena-ready: Tough, capable, ready for confrontation. "She's arena-ready—watched her break a man's arm without changing expression."

Palace-pure: Something so refined or perfect it might belong in the Crown's residence. Can be genuine or ironic. "The embroidery's palace-pure—must have cost a fortune."

Reaches

Maritime culture produces distinctive vocabulary:

Salt-sense: Practical wisdom, knowledge gained through experience rather than education. "He's got salt-sense about ships—formal training, no, but knows what works."

Tide-turner: An opportunist, someone who changes positions with circumstances. "Politicians are all tide-turners—support whoever's winning."

Harbor-rat: A smuggler, criminal, or generally disreputable dock worker. Can be pejorative or affectionate depending on context. "Half the harbor-rats in town owe me favors."

League-legal: Technically permitted but ethically questionable. References the Reaches League's flexible standards. "The cargo's League-legal—don't ask what it is or where it's going."

Storm-blessed: A lucky survivor, someone who's escaped danger through fortune. "She's storm-blessed—only survivor of three shipwrecks."

Barnacled: Stuck in place, unable to move forward. "He's been barnacled in that position for fifteen years—never promoted, never fired."

Reef-blind: Unable to see obvious dangers, navigating toward disaster. "The investors are reef-blind—can't see they're funding their own ruin."

Vent Lands

Technical and modified culture creates specialized vocabulary:

Shaped: Modified by Flesh Shaping, often obvious enough to be visible. Can be neutral or derogatory. "The shaped workers do double production—and die in half the time."

Consortium-clean: Officially approved by the Flesh Shaping Consortium, meeting their standards. "The modification's Consortium-clean—all proper documentation."

Experiment: A risky venture with uncertain outcome. Also derogatory for people with unusual Flesh Shaping. "The new business is an experiment—might make fortune, might lose everything."

Technical: Overly complicated, also a sarcastic excuse for unethical behavior. "He's being technical about the contract—meaning he's cheating us legally."

Steam-headed: Obsessed with innovation, sometimes to the point of ignoring practical concerns. "Don't get steam-headed over the design—it has to actually work."

Meat-wrong: Something deeply disturbing about a person or situation, especially regarding Flesh Shaping. "There's something meat-wrong about that place. The workers don't look right."

Far Shelf

Remote mountain culture maintains distinct vocabulary, some representing quiet resistance:

Mountain-free: Independent, outside central control. "The highland villages are mountain-free—live by their own rules."

Threadist: A resistance member or sympathizer. Self-identification among believers, accusation among opponents. "Half the village are Threadists—the other half pretend not to notice."

Hollow: Empty of pattern-control, truly free. Core Threadist concept that's entered general speech. "She dreams of hollow places where the Loom can't reach."

Saint's work: Resistance activity, actions supporting the Hollow Saints. Coded speech. "He's doing Saint's work in the city—don't ask details."

Shelf-strong: Resilient, independent, able to survive hardship. "Shelf-strong people don't complain—they endure."

Valley-soft: Weak, dependent on comforts, unable to handle difficulty. Insult. "The Crown's soldiers are valley-soft—one winter here would kill them."

Profanity and Vulgarity

Strong language reveals cultural preoccupations and boundaries.

Common Profanity

Loom-fucked: The most common strong profanity, implying fate has cursed someone or something. Extremely vulgar in formal contexts, casual in lower-class speech. "The whole deal's Loom-fucked from the start."

Thread-shit: Worthless, meaningless, garbage. "That contract's thread-shit—not worth the paper."

Warden's ass: Reference to authority figures, always derogatory. "Kiss the Warden's ass if you want, I won't."

Church-cock: A religious hypocrite, particularly one who displays piety while acting immorally. Very vulgar, potentially dangerous if overheard by Church officials. "The bishop's a Church-cock—preaches poverty from his mansion."

Residue-rot: Decay, corruption, something fundamentally spoiled. "There's Residue-rot through the whole organization."

Pattern-piss: Nonsense, lies, worthless speech. "His promises are pattern-piss—never delivers."

Curses and Maledictions

Elsewhere take you: A curse wishing someone into the terrifying alternate reality. Moderate strength. "Elsewhere take you and your complaints."

May your thread fray: Wishing someone ill fortune, pattern degradation. "May your thread fray, you cheating bastard."

Correction's coming: Threat implying someone will face pattern-enforcement. "Keep talking and Correction's coming."

Thread's end to you: Hostile farewell wishing death. Strong curse. "Thread's end to you and your family."

Loom-forget you: Wishing someone erased from fate, unremembered. Particularly harsh. "Loom-forget you—you were never worth remembering."

Romantic and Sexual Slang

Intimate relationships generate euphemistic and direct vocabulary.

Relationship Terms

Threading: Courting, dating, pursuing romantic relationship. "They've been threading for months—wedding soon, probably."

Woven together: Married, permanently committed. "Once you're woven together, there's no easy separation."

Loose threads: Casual relationships without commitment. "He's got loose threads all over the city."

Pattern-match: Compatible partners, people whose patterns suggest good romantic fit. "They're a perfect pattern-match—everyone says so."

Knotted: Married, informal usage. "Got knotted last spring, first child coming soon."

Unthreading: Divorce, separation, ending committed relationship. "They're unthreading—couldn't make it work."

Tangled up: Romantically involved in complicated way, often with multiple people or inappropriate partners. "She's tangled up with her employer—going to end badly."

Sexual Slang

Night-weaving: Sexual activity, euphemistic. "They're night-weaving every chance they get."

Elsewhere touch: Unusual sexual preferences, particularly those considered deviant. References Elsewhere's strangeness. "He's got an Elsewhere touch about him—likes things most wouldn't."

Pattern-heat: Sexual attraction, desire. "The pattern-heat between them is obvious."

Flesh trade: Prostitution, whether legal or illegal. "She works the flesh trade down by the docks."

Shaped for pleasure: Someone modified by Flesh Shaping for sexual purposes. Usually exploitative context. "The pleasure houses in Vent Lands have workers shaped for pleasure—disturbing to most visitors."

Criminal Slang

The underworld maintains specialized vocabulary for operational security and group identity.

Organizational Terms

Silk work: Activity for the Silk Threads organization. "He's doing silk work now—good money, dangerous company."

Coral run: A smuggling operation, particularly maritime. "The coral run went wrong—lost the cargo and two crew."

Hollow business: Resistance activity, Hollow Saints work. "She's in hollow business—idealist, not in it for profit."

Activity Terms

Thread-cut: Murder. "The merchant got thread-cut for talking to Wardens."

Pattern-slip: Forgery, particularly of pattern-certificates. "Quality pattern-slip costs five thousand marks minimum."

Warden-dodge: Evading authorities, particularly pattern-enforcement. "He's been Warden-dodging for years—changes cities every few months."

Residue-lift: Theft of Residue. "Residue-lift is death if they catch you—worth it if you don't."

Clean weave: No criminal record, nothing connecting someone to illegal activity. "Need someone with a clean weave for the front operation."

Knotted up: Arrested, imprisoned. Criminal usage of common idiom. "He got knotted up last week—won't talk, but they're pressing hard."

Status and Role Terms

Loose end: Lowest-ranking criminal, expendable. "Started as a loose end, worked up to running a district."

Knot-man: Enforcer, someone who handles violence. "Send the knot-men if they don't pay by week's end."

Thread-Captain: District boss in Silk Threads, leader in general criminal context. "The Thread-Captain wants his cut before anyone else touches the money."

Harbor-master: Someone who controls criminal operations at a port, often but not always Coral Syndicate. "The harbor-master takes ten percent of everything moving through."

Occupational Slang

Different professions develop specialized informal vocabulary.

Thread-counter: Bureaucrat, someone obsessed with regulations and procedures. "The thread-counters delayed the permit another month."

Pattern-pusher: Church official, particularly one who emphasizes pattern-acceptance doctrine. "The pattern-pushers came through the village demanding increased tithes."

Coin-weigher: Merchant, banker, anyone focused on money. Neutral or slightly derogatory. "Ask the coin-weighers about the investment—they know the numbers."

Flesh-twister: Flesh Shaper, always derogatory. "Don't trust the flesh-twisters—they see you as raw material."

Loom-reader: Pattern-reader, fortune-teller. "The Loom-reader says next month's good for travel—worth what you paid her."

Warden-dog: A Warden, always hostile. "Warden-dogs came through questioning everyone about the texts."

Crown-servant: Government official, neutral or mocking. "Crown-servants get paid whether anything works or not."

Ship-skin: Sailor, particularly experienced maritime worker. "The ship-skins say the weather's turning—trust their sense over charts."

Food and Drink Slang

Sustenance generates its own vocabulary:

Grain-drunk: Intoxicated on beer or grain spirits. "He's grain-drunk before noon most days."

Wine-thread: Someone who drinks wine rather than beer, implying higher status or pretension. "Gone all wine-thread since the promotion."

Market-scraps: Poor quality food, barely edible. "We're down to market-scraps until the wages come."

Church-bread: Charity food from Church distribution. Accepting it confirms poverty. "Rather starve than eat Church-bread and their lectures."

Reaches-salt: Seafood, also used to mean "authentic" or "genuine." "That story's Reaches-salt—actually happened, not made up."

Festival-fat: Well-fed, prosperous, comfortable. "Looking festival-fat—business must be good."

Famine-thin: Malnourished, desperate, impoverished. "The refugees came in famine-thin—hadn't eaten in days."

Greetings and Farewells

Social interactions use formulaic expressions:

Positive Expressions

Threads be kind: Good luck wish, general positive farewell. "Threads be kind on your journey."

Smooth weaving: Have a good day, hope things go well. "Smooth weaving until we meet again."

May the Loom favor you: Formal blessing, religious overtone. "May the Loom favor you in your petition to the Crown."

Until the pattern crosses: See you again, expectation of future meeting. "Until the pattern crosses, friend."

Good thread to you: Simple positive greeting or farewell. "Good thread to you this morning."

Negative or Cautionary Expressions

Thread's end to you: Hostile farewell wishing death. Strong curse. "Thread's end to you, betrayer."

Warden's watch: Be careful, warning of danger. Can be sincere or sarcastic. "Warden's watch on that road—they're checking everyone."

Attendance sees: Reminder that observation is constant. "Attendance sees, so keep your opinions quiet."

The Loom remembers: Warning that actions have consequences tracked by the system. "Cheat if you want, but the Loom remembers."

Integration with Worldbuilding

This living language document serves multiple purposes. For writers, it provides authentic dialogue options that reveal character background, social position, and regional origin. For readers or players, it creates immersion in a world where even casual speech reflects the pattern system's pervasive influence.

The vocabulary demonstrates how Hálcovans conceptualize their world: fate as literal weaving, status as predetermined pattern, observation as constant reality. Even profanity references the structures that constrain lives—there's no escaping the system even in speech meant to reject it.

Regional variations allow characters' origins to show through word choice. Class markers in speech reinforce or subvert social boundaries. Criminal slang creates insider/outsider distinctions. All of this language serves story purposes while enriching world texture.

Further documentation needed: formal vs. informal register distinctions, historical evolution of slang, age-based language variations, code-switching behaviors between contexts.
