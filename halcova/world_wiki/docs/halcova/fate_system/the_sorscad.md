## Overview

Every person born in Hálcova carries a **Sórscad** (SORE-skahd)—a five-part metaphysical pattern that describes who they are, how they move through life, and what they're likely to become.

The Sórscad is:
- **A metaphysical reality**: Something that exists in the fabric of Hálcova itself
- **A predictive framework**: Used by The Loom to calculate probabilities
- **A religious symbol**: Evidence of Fate's hand in mortal existence
- **A social marker**: Used in law, commerce, class determination, and identity

The Sórscad consists of:
- **Five Circles**: Core aspects of a person's being
- **Ten Cards per Circle**: Archetypal patterns within each aspect
- **One Card drawn per Circle**: Creating a unique five-card combination

A complete Sórscad reads like: *Léilcrù: Lantern — Húslaimh: Iron — Cóitcai: Pact — Hálmaht: Threshold — Sórscad: Road*

This becomes that person's pattern for life—spiritually, legally, economically, and statistically.

## How a Sórscad Is Drawn

### Two-Stage Process

**Stage 1: Natal Draw (Birth/Early Infancy)**

Three circles are drawn:
- **Léilcrù** (Core): Your essential nature
- **Húslaimh** (Flesh): Your physical being
- **Hálmaht** (Death): Your mortality pattern

These represent what you *are*—the given, the innate, the starting conditions.

The Natal Draw is typically:
- Performed by a licensed pattern-reader
- Recorded in temple and civil registries
- Sometimes shared with parents, sometimes kept sealed until Binding Day

**Stage 2: Binding Draw (Adolescence, typically 13-16)**

Two circles are drawn:
- **Cóitcai** (Binding): Your relationship patterns
- **Sórscad** (Fate): Your trajectory and decision-style

These represent what you *become*—the developed, the social, the chosen.

The Binding Draw is:
- A major life ritual in most cultures
- The moment you become "legible" to The Loom as a full actor
- Often accompanied by ceremony, celebration, or dread depending on culture and class

### Predictive Power Distribution

**Why the Natal Three Dominate Early Assessment**

The Natal Draw circles (Léilcrù, Húslaimh, Hálmaht) provide approximately **75-80% of predictive power** for major life outcomes. This is why pattern-based decisions can be made before the Binding Draw:

| Circle | Predictive Contribution | What It Predicts |
|--------|------------------------|------------------|
| Léilcrù (Core) | ~30% | Identity stability, resilience, decision-making foundation |
| Húslaimh (Flesh) | ~25% | Health outcomes, physical capability, longevity baseline |
| Hálmaht (Death) | ~20-25% | Mortality risk, manner of death, relationship with endings |
| Cóitcai (Binding) | ~10-12% | Relationship patterns, social outcomes, institutional fit |
| Sórscad (Fate) | ~10-12% | Trajectory shape, decision style, path characteristics |

**What the Binding Circles Add**

The two Binding circles (Cóitcai, Sórscad) refine predictions rather than overturn them:
- They specify *how* someone will navigate life, not *what* they fundamentally are
- They predict relationship patterns and decision-making style
- They improve accuracy on social outcomes, career trajectory, and partnership compatibility
- They rarely produce "surprises"—a Natal Draw suggesting success usually sees a confirming Binding Draw

**The Childhood Assessment Problem**

How do institutions make decisions about children before Binding Draw?

1. **The Natal Three Are Sufficient for Most Purposes**: Health assessments, mortality risk, educational placement, and early intervention all depend primarily on Léilcrù, Húslaimh, and Hálmaht.

2. **Provisional Estimates**: Experienced readers can provisionally assess likely Cóitcai and Sórscad ranges based on family patterns, district statistics, and the Natal Three configuration. These aren't official but inform decisions.

3. **Conservative Assumptions**: Institutions assume mid-range Binding circles unless evidence suggests otherwise. This systematically disadvantages pattern-constrained children (bad Natal reads assumed to worsen) and advantages pattern-privileged children (good Natal reads assumed to hold).

4. **The Binding Revelation**: Occasionally, a Binding Draw reveals something unexpected—a Cóitcai or Sórscad that doesn't match family patterns or Natal predictions. These "surprises" are rare (perhaps 5-10% of draws) but culturally significant. They become stories of unexpected rise or fall.

**Why This Matters**

The two-stage draw creates a *false sense* of openness:
- "Your fate isn't sealed until Binding Day!"
- In reality, 75-80% is determined at birth
- The Binding Draw usually confirms what the Natal Draw suggested
- But the ritual maintains hope—and compliance—during childhood

### The Hybrid Algorithm: Random vs. Weighted

In a purely random system, there would be 10⁵ = 100,000 possible combinations.

But Hálcova is not purely random.

**The Draw Has Two Components**:

1. **Unweighted Component**: Truly random selection from all ten cards
2. **Weighted Component**: Selection biased by district, class, lineage, and local conditions

**The Balance**:
- Typically 2-3 circles lean heavily unweighted (individual variance)
- Typically 2 circles are locally weighted (structural constraint)
- Which circles are weighted varies by region and institution

**Example: Industrial District Weighting**

A district characterized by dangerous labor, poverty, and exploitation might show:
- Húslaimh skew: More Iron/Stone (resilient), fewer Silk/Glass (delicate)
- Sórscad skew: More Drift/Maze (reactive, convoluted), fewer Road/Ladder (linear, ascending)
- Hálmaht skew: More Threshold/Blade (violent ends), fewer Rest/Fade (peaceful endings)

Individuals still vary, but as a population, the district's patterns cluster in "riskier" territory.

**The Feedback Loop**:

The Loom sees these patterns and calculates higher risk for the district. This justifies:
- Higher insurance rates
- Lower credit access
- More aggressive policing
- Fewer investments

These conditions create the very outcomes the patterns "predicted," which The Loom reads as confirmation.

This is not a bug. This is how the system maintains itself.

## Pattern Families

People don't recite all five cards in conversation. They reference **families**—recognized combinations with cultural names.

### Example Families

**"Steadfasts"**
- Lantern Léilcrù, Iron Húslaimh, Pact Cóitcai, Threshold Hálmaht, Road Sórscad
- Seen as: reliable, predictable, good workers, unimaginative
- The Loom rates them: low variance, low ceiling, low risk

**"Knot-Born"**
- Wound Léilcrù, Stone Húslaimh, Thorn Cóitcai, Blade Hálmaht, Maze Sórscad
- Seen as: trouble, criminals, dangerous
- The Loom rates them: high variance, unpredictable, high risk
- In their own communities: survivors, "our people," those who endured

**"Storm-Touched"**
- Spark Léilcrù, Glass Húslaimh, Twin Cóitcai, Threshold Hálmaht, Break Sórscad
- Seen as: brilliant but unstable, romanticized in stories, distrusted in institutions
- The Loom rates them: extreme variance, difficult to employ, interesting to speculators

**Why Families Matter**:

- They become stereotypes in law, hiring, and everyday prejudice
- Policies target families, not individuals
- Pattern-arbitrageurs look for "mis-priced" families—people whose actual capability exceeds their family's reputation

## Interactions with The Five Arts

### Core Binding and Léilcrù

When you bind an echo, you're trapping their Léilcrù pattern in your anchor.

- The echo retains its original pattern (which is why it still thinks it's alive)
- **Bleed** occurs because carrying another's pattern contaminates your own
- Long-term binders show "layered" Léilcrù—ghost-traces of everyone they've carried
- The Loom reads them as "pattern-unstable"

**Strategic Implications**:
- Some binders deliberately acquire echoes with complementary patterns
- "Pattern-balancing" is a trade—acquiring specific echoes to shift your own Loom-profile
- Illegal, of course, but widely practiced

### Flesh Shaping and Húslaimh

Flesh Shaping draws from your Húslaimh circle.

- Homunculi have no Sórscad (never born, never drawn)
- But they're made from your Húslaimh, carrying echoes of your flesh-pattern
- Long-term Shapers show "erosion" in their Húslaimh—The Loom sees them hollowing out
- Eventually they're more pattern-absence than pattern-presence in that circle

**Strategic Implications**:
- Homunculi are invisible to The Loom (no pattern to track)
- Valuable for operations requiring prediction-avoidance
- Shapers themselves become increasingly predictable as their Húslaimh simplifies

### Pact Summoning and Léilcrù (Consumption)

Pact Summoning consumes your Léilcrù—the same circle Core Binding traps.

- Elsewhere entities eat your core-pattern in installments
- As you pay, your Léilcrù *fades*—The Loom reads you as "pattern-light"
- Heavily-indebted summoners barely register as people in the system
- **Substitution** works because entities want *pattern-weight*, not specifically yours

**Strategic Implications**:
- Pact summoners disappear from predictions as they pay their debts
- They become "fate-attenuated"—hard to target, hard to predict, hard to hold accountable
- Some seek this deliberately as a form of freedom from the system

### Fate Threading and Sórscad (Direct Harvest)

Fate Threading directly harvests the Sórscad circle.

- Each extraction severs a branch of your fate-pattern
- The Loom literally sees the narrowing—where there were many paths, now there are few
- Depleted threaders have Sórscad circles that look "pruned"
- Extracted selves carry copies of the branch they came from, disconnected from the main web

**Strategic Implications**:
- Threaders are vivid to The Loom initially (rich patterns)
- As they extract, they become simpler—fewer variables
- Paradoxically, depleted threaders become *more* predictable in some ways
- But their extracted selves create multiple instances of pattern-fragments, confusing tracking

### Colony Hosting and Húslaimh (Transformation)

Colony Hosting transforms the Húslaimh circle.

- The colony doesn't have a Sórscad, but it has *memory*—patterns of everything consumed
- Long-term hosts show "hybrid patterns"—their Húslaimh becomes illegible
- They read as "pattern-contaminated" or "multi-sourced"
- Ancient colonies have accumulated enough patterns to constitute their own fate-entities

**Strategic Implications**:
- Hosts become hard to predict—The Loom wasn't built for hybrid beings
- Some institutions exclude them; others see opportunity in unpredictability
- Colony-pattern interactions with The Loom are poorly understood and actively studied

## Legal, Economic, and Social Uses

### Law

Sórscad patterns appear on:
- Case files
- Sentencing recommendations
- Enforcement warrants
- Contract validity assessments

Judges routinely cite patterns: "A Wound-Thorn-Blade combination suggests elevated risk of recidivism."

The circularity is obvious to critics, invisible to believers.

### Economics

Lenders, employers, and insurers use patterns to:
- Set interest rates
- Determine creditworthiness
- Make hiring decisions
- Price insurance premiums

District-level patterns influence investment: "The Foundry Quarter trends Storm-Touched—unstable returns, high variance."

Pattern-arbitrage is legal. Pattern-fraud (misrepresenting your Sórscad) is not.

### Social Identity

People discuss patterns constantly:
- "Of course she made Marshal—pure Road-Pact-Court."
- "He's Spark-Break-Wild, what did you expect?"

The constrained learn to:
- Hide "dangerous" cards from outsiders
- Play into stereotypes as armor
- Seek communities where their family is the norm, not the exception

## Anomalies and Edge Cases

### Pattern Glitches

Rare cases where drawn patterns don't match observed life at all.

Official explanation: "Data noise, measurement error."

Heterodox explanation: Evidence that The Loom doesn't see everything.

### Re-Draws

Orthodoxy forbids re-draws. Once recorded, your pattern is your pattern.

Rogue practitioners claim they can:
- "Tilt" a pattern's expression
- Suppress certain cards, amplify others
- At great cost and significant risk

Whether this is real or placebo is debated.

### Dead Zones

Certain regions produce patterns that behave strangely in The Loom:
- Predictions go wild
- Historical data doesn't correlate
- The math breaks down

Orthodox response: Avoid drawing there, or redo draws in "stable" locations.

Heterodox interpretation: Places where something other than Fate holds sway.
