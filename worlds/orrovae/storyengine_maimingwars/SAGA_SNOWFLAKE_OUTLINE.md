# THEMATIC STORY SPINE

**Axis 1 — Power/Cost**: *"Every miracle has a supply chain. Every body keeps a ledger."*
**Axis 2 — Rebellion/Empire**: *"Stability cannot be achieved under external ownership."*

The Maiming Wars is the violent expression of both axes colliding:
- **Power/Cost**: Weaponized Hollowing forces permanent loss. Every faction pays in bodies.
- **Rebellion/Empire**: The underground empire tried to forget its abandoned kin. The constructs rebel because ownership cannot provide stability. The war ends when the "monsters" reveal they're coming home.

---

# ONE-SENTENCE SUMMARY

When a subterranean civilization's symbiotic healing technology is weaponized into instruments of permanent mutilation, the resulting war destroys everything—until the "monsters" on the surface reveal themselves as long-lost kin returning to reclaim a shared home.

---

# ONE-PARAGRAPH SUMMARY

In Korrgate, an underground city where every citizen hosts a bonded partner called a Säidesolu that heals any wound, someone discovers how to break that bond permanently—weaponizing the covenant that kept them safe into tools of deliberate maiming. The resulting civil war, fought under the ideology of "The Unmaimed" (the belief that those who lose their symbiotes were always inferior), tears society apart across five years. Meanwhile, the creatures on the uninhabitable surface—long dismissed as mindless monsters—begin behaving with unmistakable intelligence. As factions murder each other over doctrine, only one person notices the truth: the surface creatures aren't invaders. They're the descendants of humans abandoned above-ground ten thousand years ago when the ancestors sealed themselves below. The war ends not when one side wins, but when the "enemy" reveals they've been trying to come home all along.

# ONE-PAGE SUMMARY (The Five-Book Arc)

## Act One: The Wound (Books 1-2)

**Book 1 — THE FRAYING THREAD**: The first weaponized Hollowing is an assassination. When a political figure is killed by having their Säide forcibly ejected, the act is covered up—but a mercenary (TORVEN) protecting potential targets, a healer (SIGURD) who sees the impossible corpse, a surface runner (KAISA) who notices intelligent behavior in "monsters," an Unseeded leader (ENEKO) navigating his community through crisis, and a bureaucrat (HELMAR) who files suspicious death certificates all become entangled in the conspiracy. Helmar dies trying to expose the truth. The war begins.

**Book 2 — THE HOLLOWING PRICE**: Six months later, everyone calls it "The Maiming Wars." The doctrine of "The Unmaimed" spreads—the belief that Hollowing reveals weakness rather than causes it. Weapons become more sophisticated. Torven's company transforms from protectors to attackers, hitting weapons labs and supply lines. Kaisa's crew is massacred by surface creatures—but she alone is spared, and she doesn't know why. Sigurd pushes himself past breaking, burning out his relationship with his own Säide. Torven dies not in glorious combat, but from an infection his Säide can't reach—killed by a tiny wound in the one place his symbiote couldn't go.

## Act Two: The Breaking (Book 3)

**Book 3 — THE BREAKING STRAIN**: Year three. Everything breaks simultaneously. KRIS (Torven's successor) commands with cold efficiency, hating what he's become. SIGURD collapses—his Säide abandoning him after he pushed too hard, becoming one of the Hollowed he spent his life treating. GRIGOR (who inherited Helmar's documentation) is discovered and executed for treason, his records seemingly destroyed. The constructs—machines that process the underground's dead and maintain its infrastructure—begin refusing orders and asking questions: "Why do I remember what I never experienced?" And the surface creatures breach three gates simultaneously in what can only be a coordinated attack. Kaisa, alone among humans, tries to talk to them instead of shoot.

## Act Three: The Dark Night (Book 4)

**Book 4 — THE SHATTERED DOCTRINE**: Year four. The "Unmaimed" doctrine collapses under its own contradictions, but its death throes are violent. VASKA (Sigurd's student) runs the hospital now, continuing research that might save future victims. AINO (Grigor's successor) maintained hidden copies of the archive—the documentation survived. Peace negotiations begin between the exhausted human factions. KRIS attends as military advisor. The summit is a trap. Kris recognizes the betrayal coming but stays anyway—his death buys time for others to escape, including the enemy commander he never trusted but saved. KAISA's communication with surface creatures is discovered; she's not executed, but she's politically destroyed. The constructs, denied personhood, stop asking and start acting.

## Act Four: The Return (Book 5)

**Book 5 — THE SURFACE RETURNS**: Year five. The surface creatures mass not for attack but for migration. TAVI MWANGI (a conscript who inherited Kris's command) prepares for invasion—until Kaisa finally delivers the translation no one wanted to hear: the creatures aren't aliens. They're the descendants of humans abandoned on the surface ten thousand years ago when the underground sealed itself against a dying sun. They're not invading. They're coming home.

The final conflict isn't human vs. surface creature. It's humans vs. humans over whether to accept their kin back. ENEKO dies believing he's secured peace—killed by human extremists who'd rather burn than share. VASKA dies defending patients—killed healing a surface creature who turned out to be as human as she was. TAVI dies holding the line against humans trying to restart the war—his last act is sending the accumulated list of the dead to Kaisa. Someone should know who paid.

**Final Image**: KAISA stands at the opened gate, the sole surviving POV character, translating between the underground and the surface. In the crowd of returning surface creatures, she sees one whose seamwork pattern matches the portrait she's carried her whole life. Her grandmother. Still alive. Still human. Changed, but home.

# STRUCTURAL ADDITIONS

## Prologue Philosophy

**Foreshadowing-Based Design**
Each prologue shows something that will happen, but without context. The reader knows *what* but not *who* or *why*. On first read, cryptic and intriguing. On re-read, devastating.

- Subtle AND obvious simultaneously
- Creates questions the reader needs answered
- Rewards re-reading after the book ends
- No exposition, no explanation, just image and implication

## Epilogue Philosophy

Each epilogue should
- Provide closure for the book's specific arc
- Open questions for the next book
- Shift perspective, often from a character we didn't see
- Mark time passage to bridge to next book's opening

## Interlude Philosophy

Interludes between parts should
- Decompress after intense sequences
- Provide context through documents, logs, letters
- Shift POV to non-POV characters, constructs, surface creatures
- Plant seeds for later books
